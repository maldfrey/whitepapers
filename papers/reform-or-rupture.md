# **I. Introduction: Losing a Generation**

A growing share of Gen Z looks toward the future and sees little reason for optimism. Whether their pessimism is perfectly accurate or only partially grounded matters less than the fact that the perception itself is destabilizing. They are entering adulthood in an economy defined by unaffordable housing, stagnant wages, rising personal debt, and a political class that often dismisses their concerns as naïve. That dismissal is not merely unhelpful — it is dangerous.

History shows that when a generation feels systematically excluded, it becomes fertile ground for simple, oppositional narratives. It takes only a few charismatic figures to offer sweeping solutions that promise to overturn the system that “failed” them. These proposals do not need to work; they only need to oppose the status quo. The simpler the message, the easier the mobilization — and the worse the eventual outcomes. A generation that concludes the system is illegitimate will not preserve it; it will replace it.

This whitepaper doesn’t debate whether Gen Z’s concerns are right or wrong. It takes their presence as the starting point and moves directly to what that means for the system they will inherit. They have entered adulthood in a landscape that no longer delivers the stability and mobility earlier generations enjoyed. And because they will eventually govern the country, the question is not whether they will rewrite the rules, but whether those changes will stabilize the system or accelerate its unraveling. The goal here is to offer an off‑ramp — a set of reforms that address the failures they face without dismantling the market economy that still underpins national prosperity.

The reforms proposed here go to the root of the problem. They target the incentives and dynamics that allowed one generation to accumulate extraordinary wealth while leaving the next with diminished prospects. If policymakers offer only incremental adjustments, the vacuum will be filled by simpler, more punitive ideas — the kind that historically produce economic collapse rather than renewal.

The goal is paradoxical but necessary: advocate for radical reform in order to preserve capitalism itself. Markets endure only when they remain legitimate, and legitimacy requires that the system serve the people living within it.

# **II. The Conditions Gen Z Inherits**

Gen Z’s disillusionment is not ideological; it comes from the world they are stepping into. Across every major dimension of economic life, the system they inherit is materially worse than the one their parents entered.

Housing is the central failure. The median home now costs more than six times the median income, and far more in major metros. Homeownership rates for young adults have fallen to levels last seen in the 1960s. When the foundational wealth‑building asset becomes inaccessible, legitimacy erodes.

Wages have stagnated despite decades of rising productivity. Entry‑level jobs require expensive degrees, while stable career ladders have been replaced by gig work and precarious employment. A generation working harder for less develops no material loyalty to the system producing those outcomes.

Debt compounds the problem. Tuition has risen more than a thousand percent since the 1980s, and student debt has grown far faster than wages. Many young adults begin their working lives underwater.

Public debt adds another imbalance. Federal debt is projected to exceed 180 percent of GDP, with interest payments surpassing defense spending. Earlier generations accumulated these obligations; younger generations will service them.

Wealth concentration has returned to Gilded Age levels. The top one percent owns more than thirty percent of all wealth; the bottom half owns almost none. Young adults hold the smallest share of national wealth in forty years.

What makes this moment uniquely volatile is that Gen Z understands the mechanics behind these outcomes. They grew up with visibility into buybacks, securities‑based lending, tax‑free borrowing, and dynastic trust structures. Inequality is no longer abstract; its machinery is legible.

Finally, Gen Z has no material stake in preserving the current system. Older generations benefited from cheap housing, rising wages, and expanding asset markets. Gen Z inherited inflated housing, stagnant wages, credential inflation, and a financialized economy that rewards capital over labor. A generation with no stake in the system has no reason to defend it.

The markers that preceded past economic rewrites — the Progressive Era, the New Deal, the Great Society — are present again, and the pressures are building.

# **III. The Ideological Barrier**

A major obstacle to reform is the reflexive appeal to “business authority” — the claim that critics “don’t understand economics” or should “trust the market.” This rests on a category error. Economics behaves like a hard science only inside simplified models; once real‑world complexity enters, it becomes interpretive. The authority being invoked is not the authority its defenders imagine.

The irony is that the loudest champions of “business fundamentals” often dismiss the very disciplines economics depends on — philosophy, sociology, psychology, history — as “useless liberal arts.” Yet interpreting incentives and institutions is precisely what those fields do. Economics is not physics; it is a humanities discipline wearing a lab coat.

The track record of business orthodoxy reinforces the point. Enron collapsed because its “innovation” was accounting fraud packaged as financial sophistication. GE imploded because financial engineering hollowed out the talent, incentives, and operational excellence that once made it competitive. WeWork burned billions on the belief that a real‑estate company could be valued like a high‑growth software platform. Sears collapsed because it was run as a real‑estate holding company instead of a retailer, starving the core business while extracting value from the assets. BlackBerry lost its dominance because it optimized for enterprise features while the market shifted toward consumer entertainment and app ecosystems. Kodak invented digital photography but protected its legacy revenue instead of adapting to the technology it pioneered. Blockbuster failed because it clung to a model built on late fees despite streaming better meeting demand. If the authorities were so authoritative, why do their best examples fail so consistently?

Markets do not reward truth; they reward incentives. Timing, leverage, regulatory arbitrage, and narrative control are not moral fundamentals — they are conditions that tilt outcomes. When someone says “the market proved this is true,” they mean the incentive structure temporarily favored a behavior. That is not authority; it is drift.

Questioning business fundamentals is not ignorance; it is stewardship. If offshoring hollows out labor markets, if housing becomes a speculative asset, and if tax structures reward extraction, the public eventually rejects the system entirely. Preserving capitalism requires interrogating its failures, not defending them reflexively.

# **IV. Deregulate Housing Construction**

America’s housing crisis is not a mystery of “market failure”; it is the predictable result of rules that outlaw the very housing types that once made cities affordable. In many metros, most residential land is zoned only for single‑family homes, making duplexes, triplexes, small walk‑ups, and courtyard apartments illegal by default. That decision forces every household onto its own lot, drives up land cost per unit, and removes the “missing middle” where affordability historically lived.

On top of zoning, building codes create steep regulatory cliffs. In many jurisdictions, moving from two units to three triggers commercial‑grade requirements that add substantial fixed costs, making small multifamily projects uneconomic. Height and stairwell rules further push development toward either single‑family homes or large high‑rises, with little in between. Parking minimums then layer on additional cost by requiring expensive car storage even in walkable, transit‑served areas, embedding those costs directly into rents and home prices.

The result is a system that favors low‑density sprawl and luxury product while suppressing the naturally affordable forms that once filled the middle of the market. The federal government cannot rewrite local codes, but it can change incentives. By tying housing, transportation, and disaster‑relief funds to modernized zoning and performance‑based codes, and by using federal procurement to normalize modular and other cost‑reducing methods, Congress can make outdated rules expensive to maintain and reformed codes attractive to adopt.

The core principle is simple: the housing market does not need to be replaced; it needs to be allowed to function. Deregulating construction — especially for small multifamily and mid‑rise housing — removes artificial scarcity and restores the basic precondition for legitimacy: that younger generations can realistically afford a place to live.

# **V. Decommoditizing Housing**

Japan’s housing affordability is not cultural exceptionalism; it is the product of a system where homes function as consumables rather than multi‑generational assets. The United States cannot import Japan’s norms, but it can replicate the mechanism that keeps Japan’s housing stock constantly refreshing: a financing system that treats buildings as depreciating assets rather than perpetual stores of wealth.

In Japan, banks, insurers, and tax authorities assume that structures lose value over time. A wooden home depreciates over roughly twenty‑two years; a concrete one over forty‑seven. Land retains value; buildings do not. This creates a market where new construction is the norm, old homes are inexpensive, and supply refreshes continuously. The U.S. mortgage system is built on the opposite assumption — that the building is the collateral and appreciates indefinitely — which locks aging structures in place and inflates prices for younger buyers.

A U.S. equivalent requires a financial forcing function. Under a depreciation‑based model, banks would treat the building portion of a home as a time‑limited asset — for example, depreciating wood‑frame structures over twenty‑five years and concrete over forty‑five. Once the depreciation period ends, the building’s collateral value falls to zero for financing purposes. Only the land remains financeable.

This single change reshapes incentives. Homes nearing the end of their depreciation schedule become largely a cash‑only purchase, shrinking the buyer pool and lowering prices. Developers acquire and rebuild. Supply refreshes not through cultural preference or seismic necessity, but because the financing system no longer subsidizes the indefinite preservation of aging structures. Old homes still exist for those who want them, but the mortgage system stops treating them as appreciating investments.

The result is a market‑driven refresh cycle that expands supply, reduces speculative premiums, and lowers barriers to entry for younger buyers — all without bans, mandates, or coercive planning. It preserves private development, private ownership, and land markets while removing the distortion that turned housing into a scarcity‑based wealth engine.

This is a legitimacy‑preserving reform. Capitalism cannot remain credible if its foundational asset is inaccessible to an entire generation. A financing‑based refresh cycle restores affordability, relieves generational pressure, and stabilizes the system before younger cohorts demand far more radical alternatives.

# **VI. The Housing Stability Framework**

The Housing Stability Framework is built around a simple principle: housing should function as a use‑good first and a financial asset second. The goal is not to punish wealth or prohibit investment, but to unwind the distortions that turn scarcity into a profit engine. The framework rests on four mutually reinforcing pillars that realign incentives toward abundant, occupied, purpose‑built housing.

### **1\. A surcharge only on value above the national median.**

A progressive federal surcharge applies solely to the portion of a property’s value above the national median home price. Ordinary homeowners are untouched. High‑value under‑utilized properties, speculative holdings, and corporate single‑family portfolios become more expensive to maintain. This flips the political incentive structure: homeowners benefit from more supply, renters benefit from lower rents, developers benefit from more buildable land, and only speculative owners pay more. The result is a broad, durable, pro‑housing coalition.

### **2\. A capacity‑based divisor that rewards density.**

A property’s taxable value is divided by the number of qualifying units — each with its own kitchen, bathroom, entrance, metering, and postal designation, and occupied at least 183 days per year. This makes low‑density homes more expensive to hold and high‑density homes cheaper, turning ADUs, duplexes, triplexes, and small apartments into financially attractive options. It embeds a density subsidy directly into the tax code.

### **3\. An occupancy requirement to prevent paper density.**

Only units occupied at least 183 days per year count toward the divisor. This prevents ghost units, speculative vacancy, sham subdivisions, and paper density. If one unit is vacant, only that unit loses the benefit — not the entire building — keeping the system fair and politically stable.

### **4\. A purpose‑built rental exemption.**

Properties with at least three units, at least 80 percent of which are rented to unrelated tenants, are exempt from the surcharge. This protects apartment buildings, mixed‑use buildings, and small multifamily structures while excluding single‑family rentals and corporate SFR portfolios. The exemption ensures the system rewards real supply rather than extraction.

Together, these pillars create a self‑correcting ecosystem: high‑value under‑utilized homes become expensive, dense housing becomes cheap to hold, empty units become costly, and purpose‑built rentals remain viable. Voters gain incentives to support new housing, and developers gain incentives to build it. The system produces abundant housing without coercion.

At a deeper level, the framework restores the legitimacy of market capitalism. A system cannot remain credible if its foundational asset is inaccessible to an entire generation. By reducing extraction, expanding supply, and aligning incentives, the Housing Stability Framework relieves generational pressure before it erupts into demands for far more radical restructuring.

# **VII. Taxing Inherited Appreciation**

A core failure in the U.S. tax system is the ability of large fortunes to compound across generations without ever being taxed. The mechanism for correcting this is straightforward. Step‑up in basis — which currently erases unrealized gains at death — is eliminated, and all appreciation becomes part of the estate tax base. Wealth accumulated during life is taxed once, at transfer, closing the dynastic pipeline cleanly and without double taxation.

Folding appreciation into the estate tax base provides the enforcement backbone. Trusts, GRATs, and dynasty‑trust structures can no longer shift or erase gains; the taxable estate is simply fair market value minus original basis. Appreciation cannot be hidden, deferred, or pushed into future generations.

A 50 percent top estate tax rate reinforces the architecture. Historically, U.S. estate tax rates were far higher, and a 50 percent rate applies only to the largest transfers. For diversified portfolios, the impact is temporary — typically recovered within a decade of normal market returns. The goal is not to destroy wealth but to prevent it from compounding tax‑free across generations.

To prevent capital flight, the framework includes a strengthened citizenship‑revocation tax. All unrealized gains are taxed at the moment citizenship is renounced, including assets held through offshore structures if the beneficiary is a U.S. person. This ensures that wealthy individuals cannot escape the system through jurisdictional arbitrage.

Ordinary families remain fully protected. A high exemption — ten million dollars per individual, twenty million per couple — shields homes, small businesses, and family farms. The tax applies only to large dynastic transfers, preserving political viability and preventing mischaracterization as a broad‑based tax increase.

The framework also closes the trust and valuation loopholes that allow appreciation to escape taxation indefinitely. Transfers to trusts become taxable events, trusts must recognize gains at generational intervals, valuation discounts are limited to genuine economic conditions, and U.S. beneficiaries of offshore trusts remain taxable.

Finally, the framework carries a generational warning: this is the moderate option. If current trends continue — unaffordable housing, stagnant wages, dynastic concentration — younger generations will not propose incremental fixes. They will propose wealth caps, nationalization, or rewrites of capitalism itself. Taxing inherited appreciation is a pressure‑relief valve that preserves markets by restoring legitimacy.

# **VIII. Buyback Reform**

Stock buybacks are not a neutral corporate action. They amplify the Buy‑Borrow‑Die cycle, inflate dynastic wealth, and convert public borrowing into private asset gains. The current 1 percent excise tax is symbolic; meaningful reform requires a rate high enough to change behavior and remove the tax advantage that makes buybacks the default use of corporate windfalls.

Buybacks function as a public‑to‑private transfer. When tax cuts or stimulus inject cash into corporations, much of it is recycled into repurchases rather than wages, investment, or innovation. Reducing share count inflates stock prices and disproportionately enriches the wealthiest shareholders. Because the underlying cash often originates from public deficits, younger generations ultimately service the debt that financed private gains. A buyback tax treats this extraction as what it is: a transfer from the public domain to private wealth.

A dynamic excise tax — for example, a rate set at five times the Federal Funds Rate — restores market discipline by making dividends relatively more attractive. Once buybacks lose their privileged tax status, shareholders must recognize taxable income when capital is returned, and corporations face stronger incentives to invest in wages, R\&D, and productive capacity. The goal is not to ban buybacks but to end their role as a subsidized financial‑engineering tool. The multiplier encodes a simple test: repurchases should occur only when they are expected to outperform a five‑year compounding horizon at the risk‑free rate.

The reform also disrupts the “Borrow” step of Buy‑Borrow‑Die. Inflated valuations become collateral for tax‑free borrowing by wealthy shareholders. A meaningful buyback tax reduces artificial valuation inflation, limits securities‑based lending, and narrows the ability of the ultra‑rich to extract liquidity without realizing gains. It is not anti‑business; it is anti‑subsidy.

The same logic applies to the “Die” step. Buybacks inflate wealth, borrowing extracts it tax‑free, and step‑up in basis erases the gains entirely at death. A buyback tax complements taxing inherited appreciation by reducing artificial valuation inflation and ensuring appreciation is taxed once, at transfer.

To prevent circumvention, the framework treats economically equivalent transactions — accelerated repurchases, foreign‑subsidiary buybacks, reverse‑split pairings, and disguised repurchases — as taxable events. Real‑time disclosure ensures transparency and enforcement.

A meaningful buyback tax is a generational‑fairness reform. It discourages public borrowing from subsidizing private dynasties, restores the link between corporate success and productive investment, and closes a key loop in the wealth‑extraction engine. It is a correction, not a punishment — a way to preserve capitalism by removing one of its most distortionary incentives.

# **IX. Offshoring Incentive Realignment**

The U.S. tax code quietly subsidizes offshoring by treating every dollar spent abroad exactly the same as a dollar spent domestically. The 3/5ths‑deductibility rule corrects this distortion by allowing companies to deduct only sixty percent of their foreign expenses. If a firm spends ten million dollars overseas, only six million is deductible; the remaining four million becomes taxable profit. This makes offshoring more expensive without banning it, using price signals rather than mandates to shift corporate behavior.

The mechanism targets the real driver of offshoring: wage arbitrage. Foreign labor is cheaper not only because wages are lower, but because foreign payroll taxes, benefits, and regulatory costs are lower as well. The U.S. tax code amplifies this gap by allowing full deductibility of foreign labor costs. Removing forty percent of the deduction eliminates the hidden subsidy and neutralizes the artificial savings. It applies the same logic as carbon pricing: price the externality, and the market stops rewarding the harmful behavior.

To be effective, the rule must apply to all foreign expenses except commodities. Corporations routinely hide labor inside contractor payments, vendor agreements, service bundles, and offshore subsidiaries. If only payroll were targeted, firms could evade the rule with simple reclassification. Applying the deduction limit to all foreign‑sourced expenses — services, intellectual work, back‑office operations, professional services, and the labor component of manufacturing — closes the loopholes. Commodities are exempt because they are globally priced and not part of the wage‑arbitrage problem.

The rule is difficult to evade. Any firm operating in the United States must file U.S. taxes, and foreign expenses appear on the books regardless of how they are routed. Reclassifying labor as “services” changes nothing if all foreign expenses face the same treatment. Shifting profits abroad triggers existing anti‑avoidance regimes such as GILTI, BEAT, and transfer‑pricing enforcement. The mechanism is a lever corporations cannot escape through accounting games.

The behavioral impact is immediate. Under current law, a ten‑million‑dollar foreign expense reduces taxable income by the full ten million. Under the new rule, only six million is deductible, and four million becomes taxable profit. At a 21 percent corporate tax rate, that is an additional $840,000 owed. Multiply this across call centers, IT outsourcing, logistics, accounting, design, and engineering, and offshoring stops being the obvious choice. Domestic labor becomes more competitive without bans, tariffs, or international coordination.

This is a market‑preserving reform. It does not punish corporations or attempt to micromanage global labor markets. It simply removes a distortion that made foreign labor artificially cheap and domestic labor artificially expensive. By correcting the incentive structure, the 3/5ths rule reduces extraction, restores legitimacy, and relieves generational pressure — stabilizing capitalism rather than undermining it.

# **X. Gerontocracy Reduction**

The United States faces a governance problem: its political leadership is aging far faster than the population it represents. This is not a cultural grievance but an institutional mismatch between modern demographics and mid‑20th‑century constitutional assumptions. A constitutional amendment linking eligibility for federal office to Social Security’s full‑benefit age offers a principled, non‑punitive way to restore generational turnover.

Under this rule, an individual becomes ineligible to serve in federal elected office once they reach the age at which they qualify for full Social Security retirement benefits. Because this age is already defined in federal law and periodically adjusted to reflect demographic and economic realities, the amendment creates a floating, neutral standard that evolves naturally over time. It applies uniformly to the President, Vice President, Senators, and Representatives, avoiding the appearance of targeting any particular office.

This approach solves several problems at once. It ensures predictable leadership refresh, reduces the risk that age‑related decline undermines governance, and aligns public service with the same retirement expectations that apply to every worker in the country. Unlike fixed age caps — “no service after 70” or “no service after 75” — this standard is harder to frame as discriminatory because it relies on an existing, universally applied federal benchmark.

The amendment also creates a powerful incentive alignment. If lawmakers wish to extend their own eligibility to serve, they must raise the Social Security full‑benefit age for everyone — a change that would require public justification and political accountability. For the first time, institutional self‑interest would reinforce long‑term Social Security solvency rather than undermine it.

This reform fits squarely within the broader theme of generational legitimacy. It updates a core democratic rule without vilifying older Americans, avoids punitive framing, and relies on standards the country already recognizes. Most importantly, it treats gerontocracy as an institutional misalignment rather than a cultural conflict. Systems that fail to evolve lose credibility; systems that modernize remain stable across generations.

# **XI. Conclusion: Reform or Rupture**

The United States is approaching a legitimacy threshold. Gen Z is not losing faith in capitalism because of ideology or cultural drift; they are responding to conditions that *appear* exclusionary from their vantage point. Housing feels inaccessible, wages feel stagnant, debt loads feel unmanageable, wealth appears consolidated, and the political system seems increasingly out of alignment with the population it governs. A system that is widely perceived as failing to deliver mobility cannot expect loyalty from those inheriting it.

The danger is not youthful impatience — it is structural drift. When a generation concludes that the rules are rigged, they do not preserve the system; they replace it. History shows that once legitimacy collapses, political space opens for simple, oppositional narratives that promise catharsis rather than competence. The question is not whether structural change is coming, but whether that change stabilizes the system or accelerates its unraveling.

The reforms outlined in this whitepaper are designed as stabilizers. They reduce extraction, expand opportunity, and realign incentives so that markets can function in ways that *look* coherent and navigable to younger cohorts. Deregulating housing construction removes artificial scarcity. A financing‑based refresh cycle makes the housing market *appear* less zero‑sum. A tax architecture that rewards density and occupancy signals that supply expansion is institutionally supported. Closing dynastic loopholes ensures that appreciation is taxed once rather than disappearing entirely. A meaningful buyback tax restores visible discipline to corporate finance. The 3/5ths‑deductibility rule corrects wage‑arbitrage incentives that make domestic labor feel structurally disadvantaged. And aligning federal office eligibility with Social Security’s full‑benefit age modernizes governance in a way that *appears* fairer and more representative to younger generations.

None of these reforms require confiscation, nationalization, or ideological realignment. They operate through incentives, eligibility rules, and tax structures — the traditional tools of a system that corrects itself through institutional evolution rather than rupture. They are moderate in design, market‑compatible in function, and stabilizing in effect because they address the *perception* of drift as much as the drift itself.

The alternative is not the status quo. If current trends continue, younger generations will not debate marginal tax rates or zoning reform; they will debate wealth caps, asset nationalization, and structural rewrites of the economic order. The proposals in this whitepaper are not the extreme option — they are the last moderate option.

A society cannot remain stable when its foundational asset appears inaccessible, when its labor markets appear to reward arbitrage over productivity, when its tax code appears to subsidize dynasties, and when its political institutions appear frozen in a demographic past. Systems that fail to evolve do not endure. Systems that adapt — deliberately, coherently, and with an eye toward perceived legitimacy — remain resilient across generations.

The path forward is clear. The United States can preserve capitalism by modernizing it, or it can allow drift to continue until the next generation replaces it. Reform or rupture. Evolution or collapse. The proposals in this whitepaper offer a way to choose the former — to build a system that *appears more fair, more navigable, and more legitimate* to those inheriting it, regardless of whether their grievances are objectively correct.
